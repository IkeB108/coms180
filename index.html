The Six-Point Checklist
for Faculty Job Satisfaction

About

The Six-Point Checklist is a guide for leaders in educational organizations of all kinds, to foster an environment where faculty feel that their work is meaningful, satisfying, and valued.

The six points draw from peer-reviewed research on job satisfaction, meaningfulness in work, and burnout, as well as interviews with two anonymous professors.

Please note that the quote blocks from the interviews with professors are paraphrased instead of direct quotations. To read a full paraphrased transcript of the interviews, click here.

When any organization is undergoing significant changes, it's important for those changes to be communicated effectively. Effective change-communication is "relevant, timely, complete, understandable, accurate, and reliable."[2] It also acknowledges employees' concerns.

Effective change-communication is correlated with perceived meaningfulness of work, which is in turn correlated with less depression and less stress.[2]

One example of a situation where effective change-communication was essential was the Covid-19 pandemic, where the future of most organizations became uncertain with stay-at-home orders.

Supportive communication in the workplace can help mitigate role ambiguity, which in turn improves feelings of job satisfaction and occupational commitment.[3]

One example would be exercises in acknowledging faculty's strengths and positive affirmations.

An inherent challenge with teaching is grappling with the fact that you won't always know how to best help some of your students (or even, whether you can). This is just one example of a reason to exercise reassuring communication with faculty.

Another avenue to consider is rethinking the systems for student evaluations.


Anonymous Professor

I find student evaluations to be concerning. In theory, they sound ideal for measuring instructors, but they‚Äôre especially precarious for professors who are women and women of color. Their character and personhood are evaluated more so than their teaching. Our teaching is depending on how students are giving this information.

Systems for student evaluations should strive to encourage fair, objective, constructive feedback, and reduce hositility, aggression, and bias.

The ideal changes that should be made to student evaluations will vary from one educational institution to the next. However, a good place to start is to brainstorm changes by taking inspiration from a statistical analysis of previous student evaluations. Previous student evaluations could be analysed to answer questions like:

How consistent is students' feedback of a particular professor?

Are things like word choice, tone, length, or subject correlated with an instructor's demographics, like race, gender, or age?

Are there any correlations between students' performance in the class, and what they write in evaluations?

One example of a change that could be informed by the data is to have students answer some evaluative questions at the beginning and end of the course, to see how much their opinions are affected by first impressions. But there are plenty of creative approaches that could be explored.

And crucially, the data analysis could serve as important context for supervisors when student evaluations are factored into the reviewing process.

At the end of the day, educational institutions have limited control over what students say about their instructors. But what they have more control over is the feedback provided by people employed by the organization.

Organizations could place a greater emphasis on supplementing student evaluations with other feedback that is rigorously moderated to help ensure that it‚Äôs fair, constructive, objective, helpful, and supportive.

Of course, educational organizations have no control over what incoming students learn at the schools they previously went to. However, organizations can provide incoming students with supplemental materials or assignments (mandatory or optional) that give students a basic understanding of the expectations that instructors will have, all before instructors even meet the students.

Examples could include books to read, study guides, practice exams, writing assignments, workshops for grammar and spelling, or for Elementary, Middle, or High school levels, vocabulary lists.

This takes some of the burden of bringing students up to speed off of the shoulders of instructors.

Engaging curriculum encourages students to build personal connections with professors, which is known to contribute to job satisfaction and feelings of personal accomplishment.[3]

Additionally, it's been shown that workers feel that their work is important when it's perceived as essential by others.[4] So when students have a deeper appreciation for the importance and value of the material, it helps instructors feel that their work is meaningful, essential, and impactful.

There are two important notes here: firstly, fostering student engagement is challenging, and it may require new, creative, out-of-the-box ideas, and experimentation. But finding ways to overcome these challenges should be a very high priority. Students learn more when they are more invested in what they're learning. This is pivotal for both the students and the instructors.

Secondly, professors already have some agency with how they structure their course. However, expecting them to come up with creative, out-of-the-box methods for making the curriculum engaging would be a heavy burden to place on them, especially when they are restricted by rules that limit their options.

This final point is an umbrella category for solutions which involve substantial funding.

When college students have more affordable tuition, more affordable living expenses such as housing and meal plans, and more affordable educational expenses such as textbooks, it enables them to live closer to campus, to work less, to have more time and energy to dedicate to schoolwork, to be more physically and mentally present in class, and to form more meaningful, more personal connections with professors. These factors contribute to increased job satisfaction and perceived meaningfulness of work for professors, and decreased burnout.
